Fair Content Attribution in the Open Internet

“On the one hand information wants to be expensive, because it's so valuable. The right information in the right place just changes your life. On the other hand, information wants to be free, because the cost of getting it out is getting lower and lower all the time. So you have these two fighting against each other.”

-Stewart Brand

Given the exponential rate of content creation being hosted in our media platforms over time, fair attribution of work and authorship of original content is stressing our Intellectual Property laws like never before. Costly patent disputes keep clashing with the throughput and speed of information dissemination in our current era.

Pinterest   200 million active users   /month

Instagram   4.2 billion likes   /day

YouTube   300 hours of video   /min

Source

The recently infamous Instagram account @fuckjerry exemplifies the tremendous discontent that these issues can create.

Among all of this new content, the line between originality and fraudulence faints, especially when there are money and recognition at stake. The ease of access to Digital Property in our current era puts the famous free-rider problem under a new lens, where original content creators can claim authorship after their creations have gained popularity but have a harder time claiming economic compensation. Individuals who benefited from these disputed public online resources gave them exposure, attention, and incurred positive externalities that elevated this content but are hard to track and quantify at the massive scale of our global networks.

Not so fast!

While it might sound possible for our future communication networks to just put a name tag on every single packet of information we create, the consequences of this public disclosure of our online activity would clearly compromise our individual privacy. Privacy is an essential part of the creative process, to begin with. Privacy gives us a break from outside influences to foster our ideas and let our personal thought processes emerge with originality. Thinking that you have nothing to hide can induce you to have nothing to offer!

If we were to forbid every shred of individual privacy to solve the problem of Fair Attribution of Work once and for all, the world would become the dullest place in the Universe.

Creativity and originality thrive from challenging the established rules, it gives meaning to them! Given the current state of content attribution laws, where patent trolls can profit by suing for copyright infringement but not by producing goods and services, where simply reposting on Instagram can land you million-dollar marketing contracts, I'd say it's actually necessary to stress our current rules a bit.

Unfairness isn't something we can or should eradicate, it's a very human feeling that indicates us when things don't seem right. Stepping on each other's work is to be expected in this noisy world, but our planet is already unfair enough to not be thankful and considerate for the creative efforts of our fellow humans.

So, don't wait to be coerced into giving credit to your peers because Fair Attribution of Work was never meant to be solved, but to be sought!

Javier Prado, 4/15/2019
